# Haiti Shines In Spite of Itself: The Soft Power of a Nation Its Own Government Ignores

*By KreyòlGenius · AYITI INTEL · April 23, 2026*

---

In diplomacy, **soft power** — a concept theorized by American political scientist Joseph Nye in the 1990s — refers to a state's ability to influence the world not through military force or economic coercion, but through attraction: its culture, its values, its achievements, its image. It is the prestige France draws from its cuisine, that South Korea draws from its pop music, that Brazil draws from its football.

Haiti has this soft power. In abundance. The problem is that Haiti — as a state — does not know it, does not cultivate it, and does not use it.

---

## What Soft Power Actually Means

Soft power is not communications or marketing. It is real influence built on three pillars:

1. **Culture** — arts, music, cinema, cuisine, fashion
2. **Values** — history, symbols, ideals carried onto the international stage
3. **Human achievement** — athletes, intellectuals, entrepreneurs who represent the country before the world

When a country masters these three levers, it earns what diplomats call "goodwill capital": other nations listen to it, respect it, trust it — before a single ambassador opens their mouth.

---

## Haiti in 2026: A Soft Power That Exists Without a State

Against all odds, 2026 has been a year of remarkable international visibility for Haiti. Not because of the government. Despite it.

### The Milan-Cortina Winter Olympics

For the first time in its history, Haiti sent **two athletes** to the Winter Olympic Games. Richardson Viano, born in Croix-des-Missions and raised in the French Alps, competed in the giant slalom — his second Olympics after Beijing 2022. Stevenson Savart became the first Haitian cross-country skier ever to compete at the Games. Both men carried the blue and red flag across Italian snow, before the cameras of the entire world.

Then came **Ralf Etienne**, 36, who lost his left leg in the 2010 earthquake. He represented Haiti at the Winter Paralympic Games — an absolute first in Haitian history. His arc — from the rubble of Port-au-Prince to the alpine slopes of Cortina — went viral worldwide.

Three athletes. Zero national winter sports infrastructure. Zero government program. They did it entirely on their own, with support from international organizations.

### Ariana in Lomé, Togo

In April 2026, **Ariana Milagro Lafond**, a 19-year-old Haitian with 15 million TikTok followers, won the 8th edition of the *House of Challenge* in Lomé — a pan-African leadership and entrepreneurship competition. She defeated content creators from Nigeria, Cameroon, Côte d'Ivoire, Gabon, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Togo, and seven other countries.

Her project: a program combining child nutrition with vocational training for Haitian youth. The global Haitian diaspora mobilized behind her with an intensity rarely seen — over one million TikTok gifts sent during her live battles. She won the equivalent of $50,000, a car, and a trip to China.

In her victory speech, she called for peace in Haiti. The Haitian government issued no official statement.

### Patrick Eugène × Dior

Haitian-American artist **Patrick Eugène** was selected from only 10 international artists — and just 2 Americans — to reimagine the Lady Dior bag for the 10th anniversary of the *Dior Lady Art* project. His "Pearl of the Antilles" collection infuses Haitian landscapes, raffia weaving, the blue of Jacmel, and the country's textile traditions into one of the most recognized luxury objects in the world. The bags were presented at Art Basel Paris, New York, Los Angeles, and Miami.

### Michaël Brun and Haitian Music

DJ and producer **Michaël Brun**, the most internationally prominent figure in Haitian music, announced his *BAYO 10th Anniversary* world tour for 2026 — with dates at Agganis Arena in Boston and three nights at Brooklyn Paramount in New York. For a decade, BAYO has been one of the most effective entry points for introducing Kompa and Haitian sounds to global audiences.

### Recognition in Rome

On April 15, 2026, two young Haitian leaders were honored at a ceremony at the **Palazzo Valentini** in Rome, in the presence of the international community. A quiet recognition, but symbolically powerful: Haiti mentioned in the European capital of history — for its youth, not its crises.

## The Real Benefits of Soft Power for Haiti

If the Haitian state decided tomorrow to take its soft power seriously, the gains would be concrete and measurable.

**Diplomatic credibility.** A country that inspires creates natural ambassadors everywhere in the world. Every Haitian who excels abroad is an unpaid lobbyist for Haiti's interests before governments, organizations, and international donors.

**Economic attractiveness.** Soft power draws investment, cultural tourism, and academic partnerships. Haitian culture — naïve art, Vodou, Kompa, Creole cuisine — is an export product that has never been seriously industrialized.

**Counter-narrative against the crisis.** Every time an Ariana wins in Africa, every time a Ralf Etienne races down an alpine course, the dominant narrative — "Haiti = failed state" — cracks. These stories do not make the crisis disappear. But they remind the world, and Haitians themselves, that the country is more than its gangs and its earthquakes.

**Identity resilience.** A people that sees its representatives shine on world stages maintains a reserve of national pride that neither economic misery nor violence can fully erode. That capital is political: it can translate into civic engagement, resistance, and reconstruction.

---

## The Real Limits: Why Soft Power Alone Is Not Enough

Honesty is required here. Soft power without a state behind it has hard ceilings.

**It is diasporic, not national.** Richardson Viano was raised in Italy. Patrick Eugène is based in Atlanta. Ariana is in Haiti but acts alone. Haiti's 2026 soft power is produced by diaspora individuals or citizens who received zero institutional support. When these individuals shine, Haiti benefits from their image — but cannot capitalize on it structurally, because no state mechanism exists to do so.

**It does not convert into foreign policy.** Soft power opens doors. But you still need diplomats behind you to walk through them. Haiti's diplomatic apparatus is underfunded, politically unstable, and incapable of turning cultural momentum into trade agreements, educational partnerships, or durable international support.

**It does not repair institutions.** A Dior "Pearl of the Antilles" bag does not build a hospital in Cité Soleil. A Paralympic podium does not restart the National Police Academy. Soft power improves the image. It does not replace public policy.

**It can be appropriated without return.** Countries like Kenya, Canada, France, and the United States benefit from the Haitian diaspora on their soil — its taxes, its skilled labor, its creativity — without Haiti receiving a systemic dividend in exchange.

---

## The Missed Opportunity: The Haitian State and Its Ghost Soft Power

There is no Haitian equivalent of the *British Council*, the *Institut Français*, the *Korean Cultural Center*, or the *Casa do Brasil*. No cultural promotion agency abroad. No national policy to leverage Haitian heritage as a diplomatic asset. No cultural attaché program in embassies. No dedicated fund to support Haitian artists, athletes, or influencers who represent the country internationally.

The provisional government has no functioning Minister of Culture with a real budget. The Transitional Presidential Council, consumed by political and security survival, cannot or will not invest in what it perceives as a "luxury."

This is a short-term strategic mistake. Countries that invested in their soft power during periods of crisis — South Korea after 1997, Rwanda after the genocide — harvested the fruits decades later in the form of tourism, investment, and international credibility.

---

## What a Visionary Haitian Government Should Do

Without claiming to offer an exhaustive program, here are five concrete and realistic steps:

- **Create a Bureau of Cultural Diplomacy** attached to the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, with a clear mandate: identify, support, and amplify Haitians who excel internationally.
- **Institutionalize Flag Day (May 18) as a global soft power moment** — with coordinated events at embassies, in partnership with the diaspora.
- **Establish a support fund for Haitian athletes** who represent the country in international competitions with zero state backing.
- **Sign cultural co-production agreements** with African, Caribbean, and Latin American countries — Ariana's victory in Togo shows there is an African audience for Haitian culture ready to be cultivated diplomatically.
- **Protect and promote Kompa, naïve art, and Haitian cuisine** as exportable cultural heritage, on the model of France's gastronomy policy or Jamaica's approach to reggae.

---

## Conclusion: Haiti Shines. Haiti Wastes.

Haiti's soft power in 2026 is real, alive, and internationally visible. It shows up on the alpine slopes of Italy, on a stage in Lomé, in the Dior windows of Paris, in arenas in Boston and Brooklyn, on social media where 15 million people follow a young Haitian woman who asked for peace for her country.

This soft power is produced by extraordinary individuals — without a safety net, without a state behind them.

That is Haiti's fundamental contradiction: a nation whose capacity to attract the world is inversely proportional to its government's capacity to capitalize on it.

Haiti shines in spite of itself.

The question is not whether Haiti has soft power. The question is: how much longer will we let it burn alone, while no one in the government thinks to tend the flame?
